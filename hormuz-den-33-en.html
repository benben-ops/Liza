Historical analogies
Analogy 1 — Arab OPEC oil embargo 1973–74
What happened
Yom Kippur War (October 1973). Egypt + Syria attack Israel. OAPEC imposes embargo on the US, Netherlands, Japan, Canada. Production cuts of −4.5 mb/d (~7% of global supply). Oil price +300% in 6 months (from 3 to 12 USD/bbl). Embargo lasted 5 months (Oct. 1973 – Mar. 1974).
Consequences
Stagflation in the US for a decade. US GDP −3% (1974–75). Inflation 12.3% (1974). Unemployment doubled to 9%. Establishment of IEA (1974). US creates SPR. France → massive nuclear program. Japan → energy diversification. Economic impact lasted into the late 1970s.
Key DIFFERENCES compared to 2026
1973: −4.5 mb/d. 2026: Hormuz blocks 20 mb/d — 4.4× larger absolute shock. 1973: the West primarily affected. 2026: the most vulnerable is the Asian manufacturing belt (84% of Hormuz oil goes to Asia). 1973: embargo came after the war ended. 2026: blockade runs concurrently with an active war with no clear end. 1973: coordinated OPEC political action. 2026: unilateral Iranian military decision.
Analogy 2 — Tanker War & Operation Earnest Will 1987–88
What happened
Iran–Iraq War 1980–88. Both sides attack tankers in the Persian Gulf ("Tanker War"). Iran mines waters, attacks Kuwaiti tankers. US launches Op. Earnest Will: Kuwaiti tankers re-flagged under US flag, escorted by US Navy. 1988: Op. Praying Mantis — US destroyed Iranian warships after a mine attack on the US frigate USS Samuel B. Roberts.
Consequences
Selective disruption of shipping (not a total blockade). Oil prices unstable but without catastrophic shock. Iranian naval capacity dramatically weakened after Praying Mantis. The strait remained partially functional throughout the entire 8-year war. US military presence in the region became permanent.
Key DIFFERENCES compared to 2026
1987: selective attacks on tankers, the strait was never fully shut down. 2026: de facto total shutdown (5 ships/day vs. 138 normally = 96% drop). 1987: US escorts tankers directly and successfully. 2026: asymmetric drones and mines (cheap, mass-produced, hard to neutralize) make simple escort impossible. The technological shift makes 2026 a qualitatively different problem. 1987: the Iraqi side compensates for the Iranian blockade. 2026: no compensating party — Saudi/UAE bypass only 2.6 mb/d out of 20 mb/d.

✅ Solution 1 — "Hormuz Protocol": International strait governance

A multilateral agreement US–Iran–China–India–EU–UN formalizing "free but coordinated" Hormuz transit. Model: Panama Canal (international governance with sovereign participation). IMO as transit rules administrator. Iran gets a formal seat in the governing body without monopoly. A neutral UN monitoring mission aboard ships.
Why it works
Addresses Iran's demand for "recognition of sovereignty" diplomatically — Iran gets a formal role without the US recognizing de jure Iranian control. China has a strong motivation to pressure Iran (45% of its oil via Hormuz). India can mediate (Op. Urja Suraksha demonstrated capability). The UK summit (3.4.) and the 35-country coalition create a political base. Reduces the risk of recurrence for decades.
Implementation
Phase 1 (1–2 weeks): temporary ceasefire, partial opening for all under "Iranian management." Phase 2 (2–6 months): negotiation of a permanent protocol in Islamabad or Geneva. Actors: US, Iran, China, India, UN, IMO. Phase 3: international governance active. Precondition for the US: halting strikes on civilian infrastructure as a goodwill gesture.
