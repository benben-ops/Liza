Historical Analogies
Tanker War 1984–1988 (Iraq–Iran)
What happened
Iran and Iraq attacked oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz. The US launched Operation Earnest Will (1987): escorting Kuwaiti tankers under the American flag. In July 1988, the US accidentally shot down Iran Air Flight 655.
Consequences and numbers
451 ships attacked (1984–88). Oil: spike to $30/bbl from $10. War ended in 1988 without a decisive winner. Key: the strait remained functional throughout — both sides needed oil revenue.
Key differences from the 2026 crisis
1984–88: Iran never physically closed the strait — only attacked ships. 2026: IRGC actively blockades the entire strait for 79 days. 1984–88: no nuclear negotiations. 2026: nuclear program is the central obstacle. 1984–88: US was not a direct military party. 2026: US+Israel launched Op. Epic Fury.
OPEC Arab Oil Embargo 1973
What happened
OPEC Arab members halted oil exports to the US and Netherlands for supporting Israel in the Yom Kippur War. Embargo lasted October 1973 – March 1974 (5 months).
Consequences and numbers
Oil price: $3 → $12/bbl (+300%). Recession in US + Europe. US reserves critically low within 60 days. Accelerated Western energy diversification. Political crises in multiple countries.
Key differences from the 2026 crisis
1973: political decision (embargo), not a physical strait blockade. 2026: physical military blockade by IRGC forces. 1973: lasted 5 months, resolved diplomatically. 2026: day 79 with no agreement. 1973: Gulf oil ~35% of world supply. 2026: Asian dependence (Japan, Korea, India, China) dramatically higher.

Solutions
Solution 1 — Phased Deal via Qatar (Hormuz First)
Mechanism
Qatar mediates a three-phase deal: (1) 60-day ceasefire + partial Hormuz opening for neutral ships; (2) lifting key sanctions; (3) start of nuclear talks. Each phase conditional on the completion of the previous one.
Why it works
Respects Iran's sequencing demand. Gives Trump a diplomatic win (Hormuz opening). Phased structure reduces "take everything and leave" risk. Qatar has the mandate of both sides and functional diplomatic relationships.
Implementation
Actors: Qatar (mediator), Pakistan (Islamic bridge), China (economic leverage on Iran). Timeline: 30 days for framework deal, 90 days to implement phase 1. Prerequisite: Trump accepts the phased approach — which he has so far rejected.
Solution 2 — Humanitarian Corridor (UN + IMO)
Mechanism
International humanitarian agreement (UN + IMO) for a safe corridor for 2,000 stranded ships. Iran allows passage in exchange for: (a) safety guarantee, (b) suspension of some sanctions, (c) postponement of military action. Neutral naval forces (Indian Navy, EU NAVFOR) as guarantors.
Why it works
Iran can claim it's a humanitarian gesture, not capitulation. US gets partial relief without nuclear concessions. UN as guarantor reduces bilateral tension. Relieves economic pressure on Asia and Europe — reduces urgency for escalation.
Implementation
Actors: UN Security Council, IMO, Iran, US, India (naval protection). Timeline: 2–3 weeks to agree, immediate implementation. Limit: Iran must agree — which is not certain without another benefit.
Solution 3 — Chinese Economic Leverage
Mechanism
China offers Iran debt deferrals ($30bn+), new direct investment, and energy contracts — in exchange for opening Hormuz without a prior agreement with the US. China de facto brokers a solution outside the American framework.
Why it works
China has economic leverage the US lacks. Iran can claim it "didn't capitulate to the US." China gains diplomatic capital (analogy: Saudi–Iran deal 2023). US in the position of result recipient — diplomatically uncomfortable, but realistic.
Implementation
Actors: China (lead), Iran, potentially Russia. Timeline: 4–8 weeks. Risk: Trump could reject the Chinese deal and escalate → US vs. China diplomatic crisis on top of Hormuz.
Solution 4 — Toll System as De Facto Partial Opening
Mechanism
Iran formalizes a "Hormuz toll system" — fees for passage by neutral-country ships. India, China, Japan, Korea pay and receive safe passage. US refuses, but neutral ships use the system. De facto partial opening outside a formal deal.
Why it works
Iran gains revenue without formal concessions. Humanitarian crisis partially addressed. US can ignore it without losing face. Can become a transitional phase toward a formal deal. Reduces urgency of recession for Asian economies.
Implementation
Actors: Iran (initiative), China/India/Japan (first "customers"). Timeline: 2–4 weeks. Risk: US may consider toll payments a sanctions violation and attack paying vessels — extremely controversial diplomatically.
